Of the People, By the People, For the People: Predicting US presidential polls per state using Machine Learning¶


Jason A. Dolorso, Master of Science in Data Science
Asian Institute of Management

I. Executive Summary¶

With the next US presidential election set this November 3, the decision of whether Donald J Trump or Joseph R Biden Jr will sit on the oval office is just around the corner. Contrary to the usual election proces, United States employs an Electoral College process where electors representing each state casts the votes$^1$.

FiveThirtyEight, a leading poll analysis website, released their national polling averages ahead of the election. There are lots of presidential forecasts being published by different entities but what set FiveThirtyEight's apart is it uses aggregated polling data conducted by other firms and organizations, and also takes COVID-19 into account in their model$^2$. The site also refers to it as a snapshot of the current polling status for each state instead of a forecast.

This study aims to utilize features such as demographics, unemployment rate, approval rating, and previous election results, to create (1) a classifier to predict the outcome, and (2) a regressor to predict each candidates' percentages, based on FiveThirtyEight's polling averages per state.

Highlights¶

  • US states are likely to vote the same party that they went for in 2016.
  • Current administration's approval ratings plays a big role in predicting poll results.
  • States with lower median age are leaned towards Trump, while higher unemployment rate and asian population are more for Biden.

II. Data Description¶

The target variable would be the data for the polling average per state from FiveThirtyEight are available on their 2020 Election page$^3$:

  • trump: FiveThirtyEight poll results for Trump in percentage
  • biden: FiveThirtyEight poll results for Biden in percentage

The features used for the analysis are all on a per state basis:

  • State: Name of the US State *
  • unemployment: Unemployment rate as of June 2020 from US Bureau of Labor Statistics$^4$
  • income: Annual personal income as of 2018 from Bureau of Economic Analysis$^5$

Trump's approval rating from from Civiqs:$^6$

  • rating_approve: percentage of approve in Trump's approval rating as of Aug 2020
  • rating_disapprove: percentage of approve in Trump's approval rating as of Aug 2020
  • rating_neither: percentage of neither in Trump's approval rating as of Aug 2020

Demographics data from JHU Kaggle dataset$^7$

  • Total population: Total population
  • Male population: Total male population
  • Female population: Total female population
  • Median Age: States' Median Age

2016 Election Results from Harvard Dataverse$^8$

  • 2016_clinton: Percentage of vote for Hillary Clinton during 2016 elections
  • 2016_trump: Percentage of vote for Donald Trump during 2016 elections

Race and Ethnicity data as of August 20, 2020 from Governing:$^9$

  • Hispanic (of any race): Percentage of hispanic for any race
  • Non-Hispanic White: Percentage of non-hispanic white
  • Non-Hispanic Black: Percentage of non-hispanic black
  • Non-Hispanic Asian: Percentage of non-hispanic asian
  • Non-Hispanic American Indian: Percentage of non-hispanic american indian

* - NE-1 and NE-2 are for Nebraska and ME-1 and ME-2 are Maine. Both states have two electoral votes$^{10}$ and different outcome, hence the two entries.

III. Methodology¶

Pipeline¶

Pre-processing
The only processing required for the data is to create an outcome target variable for the classifier based on FiveThirtyEight's poll averages, with 0 if Trump has greater percentage, and 1 if Biden. Male and Female Population are also converted to percentage by dividing it to to the total population.

Scaling
To normalize the features, three scalers we're used for all models to check which ones would yield the best results: Standard Scaling, MinMax Scaling, and Robust Scaling. The results are also checked if there are no scaling applied.

Gridsearch
Gridsearch was used to determine the best parameters for the classifier and regressor models. The following algorithms are explored:

  • k-Nearest Neighbors
  • Linear Regression - (L1 and L2)
  • Logistic Regression - (L1 and L2)
  • Support Vector Machine
  • Random Forest
  • Gradient Boosting Method

Bootstrapping
To further evaluate the results of the models, bootstrapping/cross-validation is done with 1000 iterations.

Feature Importance
Lastly, the importance of each features are analyzed to see what are the main factors that determines the poll results for each state.

Setting up the Functions, Features, and the Target Variables¶

Let us first import the modules, the dataset, and the color scheme of Democratic and Republican Party that we'll need for this study. The outcome target variable for the classifier is also created by simply determining whether Trump or Biden has higher percentage of votes per state.

Both Male and Female Population are converted into percentages by dividing them to the total population.

def split(features, target, scaler = None, random_state = 0, test_size=0.25):
    
    X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(
            features, target, test_size=test_size,
            random_state=random_state)
    
    scales = {'standard': StandardScaler(), 'minmax': MinMaxScaler(),
             'robust': RobustScaler()}
    
    if scaler != None:
        scaler = scales[scaler]
                
        X_train = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
        X_test = scaler.transform(X_test)
    
    return X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test

def gs_knn(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test, cv=10, verb=0):  
    grid = {"n_neighbors": np.arange(1,20,2), 
            "weights": ['uniform','distance'], 
            'metric':['minkowski', 'euclidean', 'manhattan',]}
    knn = KNeighborsClassifier()
    knn_cv = GridSearchCV(knn, grid, cv=cv, verbose=verb)
    knn_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    return (knn_cv.best_score_, knn_cv.score(X_test, y_test),
            {'metrics':knn_cv.best_params_.values()})
    
def gs_logistic_l1(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb=0):
    
    grid = {"C": np.logspace(-3, 3, 7)}
    logreg = LogisticRegression(penalty='l1', solver='liblinear')
    logreg_cv = GridSearchCV(logreg, grid, cv=cv, verbose=verb)
    logreg_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)
   
    return logreg_cv.best_score_, logreg_cv.score(X_test, y_test), logreg_cv.best_params_
    
def gs_logistic_l2(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb=0):
    
    grid = {"C": np.logspace(-3, 3, 7)}
    logreg = LogisticRegression(penalty='l2')
    logreg_cv = GridSearchCV(logreg, grid, cv=cv, verbose=verb)
    logreg_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)

    return logreg_cv.best_score_, logreg_cv.score(X_test, y_test), logreg_cv.best_params_

def gs_lsvm_l1(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb = 0):
    
    Linear_SVC = LinearSVC(penalty='l1', dual=False)
    grid = {'C': np.logspace(-3, 2, 6)}
    svm_cv = GridSearchCV(Linear_SVC, grid, cv=cv, verbose = verb)
    svm_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)    
   
    return svm_cv.best_score_, svm_cv.score(X_test, y_test), svm_cv.best_params_
    
def gs_lsvm_l2(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb = 0):    
    
    Linear_SVC = LinearSVC(penalty='l2', dual=False)
    grid = {'C': np.logspace(-3, 2, 6)}
    svm_cv = GridSearchCV(Linear_SVC, grid, cv=cv, verbose = verb)
    svm_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    return svm_cv.best_score_, svm_cv.score(X_test, y_test), svm_cv.best_params_

def rfc(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb = 0):    
    
    rfc = RandomForestClassifier()
    grid = {'n_estimators': [10, 50, 100, 200, 300], 
            'max_depth':[1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 80, 100, None]}
    rfc_cv = GridSearchCV(rfc, grid, cv=cv, verbose = verb)
    rfc_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    return rfc_cv.best_score_, rfc_cv.score(X_test, y_test), rfc_cv.best_params_

def gbc(X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test,  cv=10, verb = 0):    

    gbc = GradientBoostingClassifier()
    grid = {'n_estimators': [10, 50, 100, 200, 300], 
            'max_depth':[1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 80, 100, None]}
    gbc_cv = GridSearchCV(gbc, grid, cv=cv, verbose = verb)
    gbc_cv.fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    return gbc_cv.best_score_, gbc_cv.score(X_test, y_test), gbc_cv.best_params_

def classify(df_features, df_target, cv=10, display_all=True):
    functions = [gs_knn, gs_logistic_l1, gs_logistic_l2, gs_lsvm_l1, 
                gs_lsvm_l2, rfc, gbc]
    scaler = [None, 'standard', 'minmax', 'robust']
    
    top_model = []
    top_acc = []
    top_acc_test = []
    top_par = []
    
    for scale in scaler:
        X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = split(
            df_features, df_target, scale)
        model = ['KNN', 'Logistic Regression (L1)', 
                 'Logistic Regression (L2)', 'SVC (L1)', 'SVC (L2)',
                 'Random Forest', 'Gradient Boosting']
        accuracy = []
        accuracy_test = []
        parameters = []

        for fn in functions:
            acc_train, acc_test, par = fn(
                X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test, cv)
            accuracy += [acc_train]
            accuracy_test += [acc_test]
            parameters += [par]

        results = pd.DataFrame({'Model '+f'(scale: {scale})':model,
                                'Train accuracy':accuracy, 
                                'Test accuracy':accuracy_test, 
                                'Best Parameters': parameters}
                              ).set_index('Model '+f'(scale: {scale})')
        if display_all:
            display(results)
        
        best = results.reset_index().sort_values(
            'Test accuracy', ascending=False)
        top_model += [best.iloc[0,0]]
        top_acc += [best.iloc[0,1]]
        top_acc_test += [best.iloc[0,2]]
        top_par += [best.iloc[0,3]]
        
    top = pd.DataFrame({'Scale':scaler, 'Model':top_model, 
                       'Train Accuracy':top_acc, 'Test Accuracy':top_acc_test,
                        'Parameters':top_par})
    display(top.set_index('Scale'))

Evaluating Results (Bootstrapping)¶

Since our dataset is relatively small, we can use bootstrapping to evaluate the results of each model for the classifier and two regressors, that yielded the best accuracy from our gridsearch.

In [11]:
boot_clf_train = []
boot_clf_test = []
samples = 40
sim_size = 1000
drop = ['State', 'outcome', 'trump', 'biden']
scaler = RobustScaler()

for x in range(sim_size):
    boot = resample(df, replace=True, n_samples=samples, random_state=x)
    oob = [x for x in list(df['State']) if x not in list(boot['State'])]
    oob = df[df['State'].isin(oob)]
    
    X_train = boot.drop(drop, axis=1)
    y_train = boot['outcome']
    X_test = oob.drop(drop, axis=1)
    y_test = oob['outcome']
    
    X_train = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
    X_test = scaler.transform(X_test)
    
    logreg = LogisticRegression(penalty='l1', solver='liblinear', C=1.0)
    logreg.fit(X_train, y_train)
    boot_clf_train += [logreg.score(X_train, y_train)]
    boot_clf_test += [logreg.score(X_test, y_test)]

V. Conclusion¶

The results show that through Logistic Regression with L1 penalty, we can predict the outcome of a presidential poll on different states in the US with 83.76% accuracy, while we can also predict the votes that Trump and Biden will get through Lasso Regression with 0.8275 and 0.7293 mean R$^2$ scores respectively.

Based on the outcome prediction model, states that goes for Trump have a lower median age, while states that have high unemployment rate and asian population are for Biden. The importance of 2016 election results and approval ratings to the canditates' vote prediction also highlights how US states are likely to vote the same party that they went for in 2016 elections, and how Trump administration's current approval ratings can provide an idea of what the poll's outcome will be on each state.

These insights can be valuable to:

  1. The Candidates - (not only presidential) can utilize Machine Learning to have an idea on how and where to focus their campaign and campaign spending.
  2. Donald Trump, in particular - as the incumbent candidate, Trump can specifically aim to increase his approval ratings on certain states especially on swing states, or those that are known to have regularly seen close contests in past elections.

On the technical side, this study exhibited how linear and logistic regression, regarded as two of the more basic machine learning algorithms, can actually provide equal or even better results than more complicated ones such as Random Forest and Gradient Boosting Method -- provided that proper data manipulation and hyperparameters are in place.
